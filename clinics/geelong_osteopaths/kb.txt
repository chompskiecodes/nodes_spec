## WHAT OSTEOPATHS TREAT — OVERVIEW

### About Osteopathy

Osteopaths diagnose and treat many musculoskeletal problems. If it's pain or discomfort and you feel it, we may be able to help. If we can't, we know the fastest way to get you to appropriate care.

Osteopaths have very broad muscular and skeletal knowledge and are trained over a 5-year period — not a 5-minute course. Training includes extensive examination by GPs and specialists on history taking and physical examination.

### Conditions Treated

Geelong Osteopaths treats the following conditions:
- Headaches
- Neck Pain
- Back Pain
- Shoulder Pain
- Elbow Pain
- Wrist Pain
- Hip Pain
- Knee Pain
- Ankle Pain

### Why See an Osteopath

Many people see other healthcare practitioners before Osteopaths and say they wished they had come first. Key differences patients report:
- We really listen to you
- Appointments are slightly longer — we are not in a rush
- We use our hands, not machines
- We look at you as a whole person to find WHY you have pain
- We don't just crack bones, massage, or give generic exercises and stretches
- We give realistic treatment programs around your goals and lifestyle
- Our practitioners don't sell unnecessary treatment packages or lock you into pre-paid plans (this happens in Geelong)

Some people describe what we do as a combination of a GP who doesn't prescribe drugs, a Physio, a Chiro, and a massage therapist.

### What Does an Osteopath Do?

In a standard consultation, an Osteopath:
- Takes a detailed case history
- Performs a thorough assessment including orthopaedic tests
- Provides appropriate manual therapy treatment based on findings
- Gives recommendations based on treatment outcome, severity, and aggravating/relieving factors

We look at how you move, including posture, work situation, lifestyle, and diet. We look FURTHER — not all pain needs stretches, not all pain needs strengthening. We ask ourselves WHY the pain is there, not just treating the site of pain.

For example, if you present with shoulder pain not from an obvious injury, we ask why. Problems like these "fall through the cracks" with practitioners who only look at the site of pain.

### How Osteopathy Compares to Physio and Chiro

Chiropractors and Osteopaths have one more year of university training than Physiotherapists, with training geared toward private practice. Physiotherapists have more training in hospital rehabilitation and post-surgical wards.

The actual anatomy and physiology knowledge is the same. The difference is in the approach: Osteopaths look at you as an individual, not a number. We don't see you as just a spine or a joint — we ask why that structure is causing pain.

Key distinction: Osteopaths are trained to think outside the box. Right shoulder pain might seem like a rhomboid muscle issue, then appear to be referred from the spine — but the real cause is a gall bladder problem. Without asking the right questions, this gets missed.

In Australia, Osteopaths are limited to manual therapy, but retain broad pathology training that helps identify conditions presenting as musculoskeletal pain that actually originate elsewhere. In the USA, Osteopaths have the same prescribing rights as a GP.

### Can an Osteopath Fix Everything?

No — but we give you perspective. For significant joint arthritis we may help direct care and manage pain, but we don't prescribe medications. If medical intervention or imaging is needed, we'll tell you and refer accordingly. Sometimes there is no precise diagnosis possible — the brain can produce severe pain with a perfect MRI, and no pain with a very abnormal one. Ask anyone with chronic pain.

## HEADACHES

### Headache Causes

There are many, many causes of headaches — not all are the same. Some require urgent medical attention. Some are solved by a few mobility stretches. Some relate to jaw grinding. The solution always depends on WHY the headache is there in the first place.

#### Muscular Causes
The most common cause. Key muscles involved:
- **Temporalis and Masseter**: react to jaw alignment, dental work, and stress. Clenching the jaw (often unconsciously, day and night) is a very common cause.
- **Trapezius**: the large shoulder muscle, often overworked in desk-bound workers with poor posture.
- **Suboccipital muscles**: small muscles beneath the trapezius, reacting to stiffness in the upper neck joints. Small nerves exit through this area and can cause one-sided head pain into the temple.

Common reasons these muscles become tight: stress, posture, dental/jaw issues, muscular weakness — often in combination. Painkillers stop the headache but don't address the reason it's there. An Osteopath can quickly identify the potential cause and help remove it fast.

#### Nerve Causes
Nerves can be entrapped as they exit the spine or pass through muscles of the spine and face. There are many small nerves arising from the upper spine region, very commonly associated with headaches — especially those closer to the skin surface. When the cause is these small upper spine muscles, the headache is usually easy and gentle to remove, and simple techniques can prevent recurrence.

#### Joint Causes
The upper neck joints (C1/C2) and the temporomandibular (TMJ) jaw joint are the most common joint contributors. The upper neck can stiffen for various reasons — some beyond what manual therapy can help. Osteopaths can feel which joints are causing issues and use massage, stretching, joint manipulation, dry needling, and mobility exercises. Joint stiffness often contributes to surrounding muscular tension, compounding the pain. Often the relief is immediate — depending on the cause.

#### Brain and Vascular Causes
"Brain" causes include blood vessel-related causes and space-occupying lesions (e.g. tumours). Most people fear the worst — the vast majority turn out to be simple, easy-to-fix causes. Vascular causes include stroke (blood vessel break inside the brain) — where the headache, if present, is usually accompanied by other symptoms like facial weakness or voice changes.

Red flag: if you experience the worst headache you have ever felt and painkillers don't help — especially with strange symptoms like facial weakness or voice changes — seek urgent medical care immediately.

#### Stress Causes
Stress is the number one issue seen on a daily basis. Stress raises cortisol (a normal reaction, but harmful when repeated and uncontrolled), disrupts sleep and recovery, changes breathing to shorter upper-chest breaths (overloading upper chest muscles), increases muscle tension in shoulders/neck/lower back, and reduces your ability to cope with pain. We always suggest ways of calming the stress response when patients present with head and neck tension.

Random causes also exist — for example, some people reliably get headaches from eating foods containing preservatives (e.g. ham). When the cause and effect is reproducible, it's worth noting.

### Headache Assessment

All headache assessment follows the same structure:
- History
- Active range of motion
- Passive range of motion
- Palpation
- Special tests

A thorough history is vital. Not all head pain has the same cause, and the answers you give guide further questions that determine whether we can help or need to refer.

Key history questions: How long have you had them? Where in your head do you feel them? How strong are they? Do painkillers work (they often don't)? Do you have visual symptoms such as flickering or sensitivity to light? Are they worse with certain neck positions? Do they wake you at night?

### Headache Diagnosis

Most headaches have a purely structural (muscular or joint) cause, treatable without imaging. However, there are several types of headache that Osteopaths must recognise and refer for diagnosis:
- Vascular causes
- Infection causes
- Medication-related causes
- Trauma causes

Sometimes a headache "seems" serious but isn't. Pain sensations differ greatly between people — what is mild for one person can be excruciating for another.

### Headache Exercises

Only applicable when the cause is musculoskeletal and diagnosed as such. For cervicogenic headaches (upper neck stiffness), basic upper neck mobility helps: upper neck traction and nodding actions to mobilise joints and muscles of the upper neck.

Typical combination of underlying issues found: tight upper/back of neck, weak front neck muscles, tight chest muscles, weak glutes/hips (from prolonged sitting). We sit so much of our days — the neck muscles are constantly holding up the head without much movement, and the glutes are switched off. General recommendations:
- Mobilise/stretch upper neck
- Strengthen front of neck
- Stretch chest and improve rotation
- Strengthen glutes/hip region
- Review desk/sitting posture setup

### Headache Relief and Prevention

Painkillers are generally not recommended unless pain is affecting sleep (masking pain to allow sleep is worth it). Painkillers mask the pain without the brain getting the feedback it needs — the problem is still there, you just can't feel it as much.

Prevention: maintain both strength and mobility. You cannot have too much of either — too much tension causes headaches, and too much mobility can also cause headaches. The goal is a combination of both.

### Osteopathic Treatment for Headaches

Treatment centres on ensuring you're mobile where needed and stronger where needed. The exact style and pathway depends on the reason the pain is there. Techniques include:
- Soft tissue techniques
- Joint manipulative techniques
- Stretch techniques
- Self-management exercises

Sometimes an indirect or cranial approach may be of benefit. The number of treatments depends on severity and other factors such as degenerative change. Sometimes full resolution comes quickly; sometimes it takes longer. The aim is to not only help you get rid of headaches, but keep them away.

## NECK PAIN

### Neck Pain Causes

The biggest driver of musculoskeletal neck pain today is device use — phones, tablets, computers. Ten years ago neck pain was present but nothing like today, and it now significantly affects young children. The weight of the head when looking down at a phone forces neck muscles into positions they're not designed for.

Causes:
- **Muscular**: often muscles appear tight but are actually weak; the brain forces tension to protect them. Strength exercises help.
- **Joint**: stiffness and degeneration cause inflammation; excessive inflammation can cause minor nerve compression. Over time, significant degeneration causes more nerve compression.
- **Disc**: too much movement in one area causes disc degeneration or prolapse — severe neck/shoulder/arm pain results.
- **Whiplash/trauma**: can prolapse discs acutely.
- **Degenerative change**: joints overused simply wear out.
- **Space-occupying lesions**: always considered during thorough history.

Some presentations can be too acute for Osteopaths to treat and require other intervention. Only a highly trained clinician can help you understand what the cause truly is.

### Neck Pain Assessment

Assessment starts with a thorough case history — questions help determine whether we can actually HELP you, because on occasion, we can't.

We then assess how you move. Questions include:
- Do you have full range of motion?
- Is it sore on bending back? Forward? On rotation?
- Is it worse when we palpate your neck?
- Where exactly is it sore? What makes it worse? What makes it better?

Sometimes special tests are needed relating to specific pathologies like disc-origin pain. No test is 100% conclusive, but they provide much better information about the possible cause.

Example: pain worse looking up-left, with radiation into the left shoulder blade and/or arm, may indicate a disc issue — different treatment pathway and possible referral for imaging.

### Neck Pain Diagnosis

Diagnosing neck pain is usually straightforward. X-rays or MRI are only needed when the result would change the treatment outcome. Examples of when imaging is appropriate:
- Older patient with very stiff neck — degenerative changes likely present
- Significant trauma, possible fracture
- Suspected non-structural cause

X-rays show bone only; MRI shows all structures (discs, nerves, soft tissue). Just because something "shows" on imaging doesn't necessarily mean it is the actual cause of your pain — this is an important point to understand.

### Neck Pain Exercises

Not all neck pain is fixable by exercises alone, and not all by manipulation — it's variable. The neck is part of a body chain. Weakness and tension elsewhere can affect the neck, just as the neck contributes to movements throughout the spine.

For example: turn your head as far as you can to the left. You'll feel your shoulders and chest engage. This is the "chain" of muscles working as a team. Sometimes it makes sense to take load away from the neck; sometimes direct neck strengthening is appropriate.

Key prevention: ergonomics at work and home, plus mobility exercises to undo tension created during sitting. Limiting device time, especially for children, protects the neck long-term.

### Neck Pain Relief

To get relief, a practitioner must look at the whole picture — not just the neck. Simply doing exercises, getting a massage, or getting the neck "cracked" in isolation doesn't cut it. The entire rear spine musculature, right up to the neck, is engaged in almost every major movement — identifying why the neck is loading the way it is requires looking at the full body.

### Osteopathic Treatment for Neck Pain

Osteopathic treatment never focuses only on the neck. Treatment includes: massage, joint manipulation, dry needling, stretching exercises, and strength work — looking at all related structures and why the neck is being loaded the way it is.

## BACK PAIN

### Back Pain Causes

Back pain is the single biggest problem facing most people today.

#### Structural Causes
An issue with one or more structures making up the spine:
- **Muscular**: often the site of pain, but sometimes the cause is a nearby joint (e.g. a stiff rib-vertebra joint feels like a muscle pain)
- **Joint**: facet joints, rib-vertebra joints
- **Bone**: fractures, osteoporosis-related compression
- **Ligament**: strained or inflamed spinal ligaments
- **Disc**: degenerative disc or disc prolapse
- **Nerve**: compression or irritation along the spinal nerve roots
- **Lymphatic system**

Pain is the brain's way of saying "change something." Despite popular belief, your back doesn't "go out of place" and can't be "put back in." If the back is stiff, there is always a reason.

#### Referred Causes
Pain felt in the spine that actually originates elsewhere. Possible sources include:
- Reproductive organs (especially in women)
- Stomach/intestines
- Gall bladder
- Heart
- Lungs

A practitioner who only looks at the site of pain is lost.

#### Emotional Causes
Anxiety and depression genuinely affect how the brain interprets pain, potentially amplifying it significantly. There are two types of stress: within your control and out of your control. It's easy for practitioners to miss stress as a contributing factor. We see the enormous satisfaction of reducing pain through addressing stress and lifestyle.

### Back Pain Assessment

Thorough back pain assessment must go beyond just looking at the back. Assessment includes:

**Active Range of Motion** — how you move yourself. Can you bend forward, backward, rotate? Does your ability to do this match what you said in the history? If there's an issue with a specific movement, it points toward the related structure.

**Passive Range of Motion** — when the practitioner takes you through range without you helping. This assesses structures that don't contract — joints and ligaments — as opposed to muscles. If something hurts during a contraction, the problem is likely muscular or at a tendon/bone insertion. Most of the time there is a combination of things going on.

**Orthopaedic Tests** — specific movements indicating pathology or ruling out serious causes.

The diagnosis depends on: the history you give, the practitioner's background, the examination technique, and any underlying pathology. Sometimes a precise diagnosis isn't possible — "non-specific back pain" is a valid finding based on thorough assessment.

### Back Pain Diagnosis

Imaging (X-ray, MRI) is only warranted if it would change the treatment plan. Evidence suggests imaging can sometimes worsen treatment outcomes — not a routine step. X-rays and CT scans show bone; MRI shows everything, including discs and nerves. Sometimes blood tests are needed to exclude systemic pathology.

### Back Pain Exercises

No one-size-fits-all solution — exercises must match what's going on and guide you away from pain. Commonly, pain attributed to back weakness is actually caused by weak gluteal/butt muscles. The actual spine rarely needs major direct strengthening for back pain to subside.

Pilates and yoga can help, but ONLY if the exercises match what your brain wants. Generic exercise sheets often make people worse when followed despite causing pain.

Prevention: strength exercises, mobility exercises, good diet, good sleep, less stress. People take their backs for granted until a simple movement (putting on a shoe) triggers an acute episode.

### Back Pain Relief

Relief comes when the brain's threat perception is reduced. A perfect MRI can exist alongside severe pain; an abnormal MRI can exist with no pain. A combination approach works best: exercise, massage/manipulation, meditation, improving gut health.

Be wary of the single-focus practitioner — whether they only do manipulation, only exercise, or only massage. Very rarely, surgery is a last resort — and thankfully, less common than it used to be.

### Osteopathic Treatment for Back Pain

Osteopaths take a holistic view — you're an individual, not a number. We listen thoroughly, gather relevant information, and guide you out of pain as accurately as possible. You're not just a spine, and you're not prescribed generic exercises like everyone else with a back problem.

## SHOULDER PAIN

### Shoulder Pain Causes

Most shoulder pain is NOT in the actual shoulder joint itself — it's in front of, beside, or near the shoulder blade, away from the joint.

#### The Shoulder Joint
A ball-and-socket joint — complex but relatively loose. Many muscles, tendons, and ligaments can cause pain. Certain movements tell us what structure is at fault. Almost always other contributing factors exist around the joint at the same time.

#### Referred Pain from the Neck
Lower neck disc issues (especially C6) commonly cause shoulder pain while the neck itself feels completely fine. Specific movements reveal this in seconds — there are tests that tell us immediately whether a disc issue in the neck is contributing.

#### Muscular Tension
Very common. Any rotator cuff muscle can cause shoulder pain from a tear (direct injury or overuse) or from chronic tension. Osteopaths look at ALL the reasons WHY that shoulder is causing pain.

#### Ligament Strains and Bursitis
Ligament injuries are usually traumatic — we pick that up in the history. Bursitis usually results from a chronic movement problem in the shoulder, requiring a specific series of steps to address.

#### Rib Joint Pain
Upper ribs join the spine at costovertebral joints that can become stiff and cause "shoulder pain." Very commonly missed and misdiagnosed as rhomboid muscle pain. Rib joint pain is the number one reason people present to emergency departments thinking they are having a heart attack.

#### Referred Pain from Organs
Gall bladder, heart, and lungs can all produce shoulder pain. Only a thorough history and assessment can check for this. If organ referral is suspected, we refer you to appropriate care immediately.

### Shoulder Pain Assessment

History questions: How long? Where exactly is the pain? Is it sharp or achy? Does it hurt on specific movements? Can you take the pain away?

Key assessment techniques:
- We can press somewhere away from the "pain" and exactly recreate your pain. This means the cause is NOT where you're feeling it.
- We can get you to activate a specific muscle BEFORE the movement that causes pain — and the pain disappears or significantly reduces. This reveals what's actually driving the problem.
- Active resisted strength tests for the rotator cuff and surrounding muscles.
- Special orthopaedic tests to determine whether referral for diagnostic ultrasound or specialist opinion is needed.

### Shoulder Pain Diagnosis

Check active ROM → passive ROM (does the brain allow us to take the shoulder through range? If not, overriding protection mechanisms suggest an injured structure) → active-resisted muscle strength → special orthopaedic tests.

Note: no muscle test is perfect. To do any test, the brain recruits multiple muscles simultaneously. The ability to see past isolated test results is what makes Osteopathic assessment different.

Most shoulder pain is relatively straightforward and successfully treatable.

### Shoulder Pain Exercises

No one-size-fits-all exercises for the shoulder. Generic band exercises given without a proper diagnosis frequently make things worse. Rotator cuff exercises are sometimes critical but sometimes a waste of time — or actively harmful. Shoulder muscles never work in isolation, so a "generic" solution to shoulder pain via exercise is pointless without diagnosis.

Sometimes movement patterns (rolling patterns, gait/walking patterns) are more effective than isolated exercises. We look for patterns and ways to help the body improve those patterns.

### Shoulder Pain Relief and Prevention

Straightforward issues respond quickly to Osteopathic treatment combinations. Bone spurs, bursitis, and arthritis may not change with a few manual therapy treatments alone.

Prevention: the primary cause of shoulder pain is poor posture. Device use from a young age promotes rounded shoulders and a "flexed" shoulder posture. Upper back flexion causes loss of rotation — the body/brain gets used to this posture over time. The rounded spine places more strain on the shoulder joint, eventually causing degenerative changes inside the joint, tendons, and bursa structures.

### Osteopathic Treatment for Shoulder Pain

Always looking beyond the shoulder. Typical treatment: soft tissue massage, joint mobilisation, dry needling, stretches, ergonomic advice. The goal is to diagnose the problem and do something about it — taking a global view as to WHY the shoulder problem is there, and providing a realistic solution.

## ELBOW PAIN

### Elbow Pain Causes

Elbow pain can be quite debilitating, especially in the dominant arm. Most results from overuse of hand flexors or extensors — not always from playing tennis or golf. Not all elbow pain is "tennis elbow"; not all is "golfers elbow."

#### Golfers Elbow (Medial Epicondylitis)
Straining of muscles/tendons involved in gripping. These insert into a common tendon on the inside of the elbow. Inside-edge elbow pain.

#### Tennis Elbow (Lateral Epicondylitis)
Straining of muscles/tendons involved in extending the hand/wrist. These insert on the outside of the elbow. Outside-edge elbow pain.

#### Olecranon Bursitis
A sore, often red swelling on the back of the elbow. Very painful. Causes include gout, trauma, and direct impact. Manual therapy has limited effectiveness here.

#### Biceps Tendonitis
The biceps inserts close to the elbow; overuse causes front-of-elbow pain — usually more to the front than the sides.

#### Triceps Tendonitis
The triceps inserts close to the elbow; overuse causes rear-of-elbow pain — more to the rear than the sides.

#### Ulnar Nerve Pain
The ulnar nerve travels down behind the inside of the elbow joint. May relate to nerve compression issues further up the arm or at the neck.

### Elbow Pain Assessment

Assessment follows: History → palpation → active resisted movements → diagnostic images if needed.

Osteopaths look for movements and positions that increase your pain, followed by movements and positions that decrease it. The actual cause of elbow pain is often away from the elbow — in the shoulder or wrist. Sometimes we can take your pain away in literally seconds, for it to return — this helps identify the true source. Osteopaths act as "pain detectives" to find WHY the problem is there so we can help you faster.

### Elbow Pain Diagnosis

Mostly straightforward — fewer structures can go wrong with the elbow compared to more complex joints. We correlate site, movement quality, and how pain can be reproduced or decreased. Diagnostic ultrasound is sometimes needed to determine whether a structure is torn.

### Elbow Pain Exercises

Few exercises are specific to the elbow — primarily flexion/extension and rotation of the wrist. The elbow sits between the shoulder and wrist; all elbow exercises involve muscles crossing at least one of those joints. Exercise prescription must account for these chain effects rather than treating the elbow in isolation.

### Elbow Pain Relief

Requires correct diagnosis. If muscular: reduce pressure on surrounding structures and limit movement. Tendon/muscle tears must be allowed to heal — continuing to use a damaged structure prevents healing. A wrist brace is sometimes required to limit hand movements that are driven by muscles crossing the elbow. Elbow pain heals slowly when people wait hoping it will resolve on its own.

### Elbow Pain Prevention

Ensure you are strong enough for the activities you're attempting. There are no meaningful general mobility exercises for the elbow — prevention comes down to appropriate load management and not overusing a structure already under strain.

### Osteopathic Treatment for Elbows

Osteopaths never only look at the site of pain. Elbow treatment can definitely involve treating the soft tissue and skeletal structures of the elbow, but also the shoulder, wrist, and neck. Typical treatment: massage, articulation, manipulation, immobility advice, and postural advice — depending on presentation. Most of the time Osteopaths can help elbow pain very quickly.

## WRIST PAIN

### Wrist Pain Causes

Wrist pain can be debilitating — the wrist is involved in so many essential movements. Slightly challenging to diagnose because there are so many structures that could be at fault.

#### Joint Causes
The wrist is made of 8 carpal bones. Common joint issues include lunate bone dislocation and scaphoid fracture. Another common cause is degenerative change of the joint between the thumb and the Trapezium bone — a lot of movement occurs here.

#### Tendon Causes
There are 17 separate muscles with tendons crossing the wrist — a high potential for tendon pain. Actual complete tears are relatively uncommon.

#### Tendon Sheath Causes (Tenosynovitis)
All tendons at the wrist run within sheaths that allow sliding. The sheath can become inflamed — tenosynovitis — which is very painful. **Carpal tunnel syndrome** is the most common variant: inflammation within the carpal canal reduces the space available for the nerve passing through, causing nerve-related signs and symptoms including tingling, numbness, and weakness in the hand.

#### Bone Causes
Fractures from falls — often the radius or ulna, usually immediately apparent. **Scaphoid fracture** (from falling on an outstretched hand) can be cracked but the fracture may not be visible on imaging for approximately 21 days after injury — easily missed and commonly undertreated as a result.

#### Nerve Causes
Nerve pain can arise from lower back injury or from compression or irritation along the nerve pathway into the hand.

### Wrist Pain Assessment

Assessment involves: History → active ROM → passive ROM → special tests → imaging if needed.

Tricky aspects of wrist assessment:
- Scaphoid fractures invisible on imaging for ~21 days post-injury
- Painful structures can be hidden beneath other structures (e.g. lunate bone under extensor muscles)
- Wrist pain can be referred from any elbow muscle that crosses the wrist joint

As Osteopaths, we think about the chain of muscles from the shoulder and elsewhere. It's remarkable how sometimes stopping your wrist pain is achieved by recruiting other related muscles up the chain — demonstrating the real cause isn't local at all.

### Wrist Pain Diagnosis

Check active ROM (all directions — does it hurt in one direction or multiple?) → passive ROM (can WE take your wrist through range?) → strength tests → special tests → diagnostic imaging if indicated.

### Wrist Pain Exercises

Wrist exercises address flexors, extensors, and rotation muscles. Some end-range mobility exercises help with range of motion. However — if tenosynovitis is present, these same mobility exercises will make things significantly worse. Proper diagnosis before starting any program is essential.

### Wrist Pain Relief and Prevention

Manual therapy may not change all types of wrist pain (bone, nerve, severe arthritis, acute tenosynovitis). Treatment is offered when it's likely to help.

Prevention: avoid repetitive forced wrist extension or gripping beyond your training capacity. Previous shoulder or elbow injuries can alter movement mechanics and contribute to wrist issues later. Progressive yoga and bodyweight loading through the wrist — practised carefully over time — can build significant wrist resilience.

### Osteopathic Treatment for Wrist Pain

Never only looking at the wrist. Treatment depends on what you present with and why — addressing the full kinetic chain from neck to hand. May include freeing related joints like the elbow that are limiting wrist mechanics. Sometimes the solution is simply a better keyboard and mouse setup, or freeing up a previously injured elbow — seeing an Osteopath shortens this diagnostic process significantly.

## HIP PAIN

### Hip Pain Causes

Hip pain is one of the most common things seen daily. People's definition of "hip" covers a large area — from the lower buttocks to the lower back, around the side, and into the front and groin. We always ask patients to show us exactly where.

#### Hip Pain in the Front
Could be nerve, tendon, ligament, joint, or labral issue. Sometimes the issue is in the joint itself or the labrum (cartilaginous ring surrounding the joint). Many possible causes requiring professional assessment.

#### Hip Pain in the Back
Mostly muscular, involving the glutes. One muscle may overwork for a weaker one — and to really decrease pain, you need to strengthen the weaker one. **Sciatica** (referred from the lower back) is common — but you can think you have sciatica and actually have referred pain from another structure entirely. Ligament pain from postural issues can be quite severe.

#### Hip Pain in the Side
Common in middle-aged and older women, especially soreness when lying on their side in bed at night. Often **trochanteric bursitis** (inflamed trochanteric bursa). This is one of the few conditions where a guided injection is advocated — the bursa is superficial and injections can be quite successful. Tight side muscles and glute muscles also refer into the hip side.

Structural causes include: muscular, joint, bone, ligament, lower back disc, and nerve — all are considered in assessment.

### Hip Pain Assessment

Osteopaths look at your hip based on the history. Sometimes pain reported in the front of the hip tests as if there's a joint issue — yet activating a specific glute muscle in a specific way eliminates that pain in seconds. So easy for clinicians to miss when not trained to look for this.

Assessment: History → reproduce pain with specific movements → attempt to eliminate pain by activating a different muscle → determine whether the cause is truly joint-based or referred/muscular.

### Hip Pain Diagnosis

Check active ROM (bending forward, to the side, squatting) → passive ROM → strength tests → assess WHY those deficits exist.

Your brain is in control at all times. Weakness in a specific way, and tension in another way, are both signals the brain uses to protect an injured area. Most hip pain is relatively straightforward and successfully treated by an Osteopath.

### Hip Pain Exercises

No generic program works for all hip pain. Mobility and stretches can sometimes make hip pain worse; activation and strengthening can too. Only a skilled practitioner assessment determines the right approach.

In western culture we don't take our hips through range enough on a daily basis because we live 1 metre off the ground — we sit too much and don't get to the ground enough. For those with no joint pathology, a general hip mobility sequence can be very beneficial — but even a generic sequence may make some people worse.

### Hip Pain Relief and Prevention

Prevention is difficult — hip pain occurs in both inactive people and highly active athletes. If there is a joint pathology, the brain creates pain, inflammation, swelling, weakness, and tension to protect the area.

Treatment: exercise, massage, manipulation, or a combination — depending on cause. Injections or surgery are rarely needed but available when appropriate. Most patients seen at Geelong Osteopaths get excellent results with a combination of therapeutic interventions.

### Osteopathic Treatment for Hip Pain

Always looking beyond the hip. Osteopathic treatment is dependent on what you present with and why. The goal is to diagnose the problem, do something about it, and look further into the reason so that results last. Some cases do require surgical referral, and when that's the only solution, we'll tell you clearly.

## KNEE PAIN

### Knee Pain Causes

Knee pain is always concerning because it's often achy and can be sharp on straightening the leg or walking. Causes largely depend on age and activity level.

#### Local Causes (in/around the knee)

**Ligament causes**:
- Collateral ligaments (inside/outside of knee) — usually traumatic, easy to locate as they're superficial
- Cruciate ligaments (ACL/PCL) — also traumatic; ACL rupture requires orthopaedic opinion, not Osteopathic treatment

**Tendon causes**: Quadriceps/patellar tendon insertion (front of knee), ITB friction syndrome (outside), Sartorius insertion (front-inside).

**Arthritic/degenerative causes**: Knee arthritis and degenerative changes under the kneecap are very common with age.

**Bursa causes**: Infrapatellar, prepatellar, and pes anserine bursas can cause significant pain from kneeling or activity.

**Nerve causes**: Sciatic nerve or L3 spinal segment referred pain presenting as knee pain.

**Meniscus causes**: Medial and lateral menisci — injury gives joint line pain and possible swelling. Occurs through wear and tear or direct impact injuries in sport.

**Muscle causes**: Vastus Medialis/VMO (front of knee), Popliteus (back of knee), Adductors (inside of knee) are most commonly implicated in knee pain.

**Bone causes**: In younger patients — **Osgood-Schlatter disease** (growth plate issue during long bone growth). In older patients — arthritis or (rarely) tumours.

**Lymphatic causes**: Fluid congestion may contribute to poor circulation and knee pain.

#### Referred Causes (away from the knee)

**Lower back**: L3 nerve root compression produces pain felt in the knee area.

**Hip**: The hip joint or surrounding muscles frequently produce pain felt as "inside the knee." The brain will give you pain in the KNEE when you've got a hip issue.

**Ankle**: The most important joint in your body. If the ankle is injured there's a very high chance the knee will have issues at some point — the bones are interconnected. An injured ankle changes loading mechanics all the way up the chain.

### Knee Pain Assessment

History (Was there an injury? How did it happen?) → reproduce pain with specific movements → attempt to eliminate by activating a different muscle (e.g. adductor activation can remove apparent meniscus-pattern pain in seconds) → determine whether imaging or orthopaedic referral is needed.

All of these assessment "tricks" enable us to determine whether a pathology needs referral for further examination or MRI.

### Knee Pain Diagnosis

Check active ROM (bending, squatting, standing from a chair easily?) → passive ROM (does the brain let us take the knee through range? If not, protection strategies usually indicate joint, tendon, or ligament injury) → strength tests (reliable? Easy or difficult?) → identify WHY deficits exist.

Combining all findings with the history, we can usually arrive at a diagnosis — sometimes immediately, sometimes after further imaging. Most knee pain is straightforward and treatable by an Osteopath quickly.

### Knee Pain Exercises

**Open chain exercises** (foot not in contact with the ground): swimming, leg extensions, leg flexion — not usually the starting point unless the knee and surrounding muscles can handle the load.

**Closed chain exercises** (foot in contact with something — ground or bike pedal): stationary bike with no/low resistance, squats, lunges — usually the starting point.

Programs range from very basic to complex progressive strength plans, building from scratch when needed.

### Knee Pain Relief and Prevention

Requires accurate diagnosis. Allow torn structures to heal — continuing to use a damaged structure prevents recovery. If there's a sore front of the knee and kneeling makes it worse — reduce kneeling. But if you're a tiler who must kneel, look at braces, guards, and protection to manage recovery time.

Key insight: if the knee is sore, walking will not be even or efficient — compensation always occurs. We look at hip, ankle, and other related structures, not just the knee itself.

General prevention when pain-free: focus on quad, hip, and ankle strength. Yoga can be very effective for both leg strength and ankle/hip mobility.

### Osteopathic Treatment for Knee Pain

Osteopaths will never only look at the site of pain in the knee. There is always a compensation pattern happening — you limp, you use your arms differently when you walk. Knee treatment addresses hip, ankle, and other related structures as needed. Referral for further tests and specialist opinions happens when needed to get you better as fast as possible.

## ANKLE PAIN

### Ankle Pain Causes

Most ankle pain is from a sprain — foot rolling inwards (mostly) on an uneven surface. Once sprained, the ankle is likely to re-sprain repeatedly because the ligaments' ability to send stretch signals to the brain is reduced — the signal takes longer, making re-rolling annoyingly easy.

#### Local Causes

**Ligament causes**: Most common cause of ankle pain. Ankle sprains are among the most common injuries humans sustain.

**Muscle causes**: All ankle muscles originate just above the ankle in the leg — all cross the joint.

**Bursa causes**: Usually inflammatory, behind the Achilles tendon. An inflamed fat pad in the same space also causes pain.

**Tendon causes**: Tendons at the ankle run beneath retinaculums or around bony grooves where they can become inflamed.

**Nerve causes**: Nerve pain from lower back injury or compression along the nerve pathway into the feet.

**Joint causes**: Poor biomechanics from footwear, weakness in foot/leg/hip muscles. Severe sprains can injure structures inside the joint — not only the external ligaments.

**Bone causes**: Fractures are usually exquisitely painful and warrant imaging immediately. Scaphoid-type bone injuries around the ankle should also be considered.

#### Referred Causes
Lower back pathology or knee pathology can present as ankle pain — always considered in thorough assessment.

### Ankle Pain Assessment

Relatively straightforward because the ankle joint has limited range. History → check all the usual ligament structures and the joint → refer for imaging if needed.

Key point: the ankle is your body's most important joint. When it can't move properly, it affects every other joint and muscle in the body — more than immobilising any other joint would. It is critically important to get ankle problems properly treated.

Assessment: active ROM (can you move in all directions? Does it hurt in one or multiple?) → passive ROM (does the brain let us take it through range? If not, protection mechanisms are indicating injured structures) → active-resisted strength tests. Osteopaths think about the full chain above: knee and hip.

### Ankle Pain Diagnosis

After history and assessment, the combination of findings determines whether treatment, imaging, or referral is the next step. Active ROM → passive ROM → strength and stability tests.

### Ankle Exercises

Three goals: reducing ankle movement (taping), increasing ankle movement (mobility), and stabilising ankle movement (proprioception/balance training — especially important post-sprain).

**Post-sprain stability exercises**: critical because the ligaments' stretch-signal response is reduced after a sprain, making re-rolling much easier. Wobble board work and single-leg balance exercises restore proprioception over time.

**Squatting mobility**: the ankle plays a huge role in squatting mechanics — mobility exercises often target this pattern.

Note: immediately after a sprain, mobility exercises typically make things worse. Taping is usually the best initial approach — solid/rigid tape following ligament lines to limit specific movements. Sometimes a moon boot is required to completely eliminate movement in severe cases.

### Ankle Pain Relief and Prevention

Manual therapy may not change all ankle pain types (bone, nerve, severe arthritis). Treatment is offered when it's likely to help.

**Prevention post-sprain**: controlled ankle rehabilitation — wobble board, single-leg balance challenges. If returning to high-risk sport (basketball, netball), strap or brace the ankle. But don't rely on supports forever — the goal is to restore strength and proprioception so supports aren't needed.

### Osteopathic Treatment for Ankles

Osteopaths never only look at the ankle — always assessing other structures as well. Treatment depends on what you present with and why. Key questions: Does the ankle need stabilising? Does it need mobilising? What combination of approaches returns you to your activities fastest? The goal is getting you back on the field or back doing what you love, as fast as possible.
