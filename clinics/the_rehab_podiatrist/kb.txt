## Services

The clinic offers a range of different services to help identify and manage foot, ankle and shin pain.

### Injury and Pain Assessment

A thorough assessment of the feet and lower limbs, ordering diagnostic testing if required, and crafting a personalised treatment plan. Alex can help with all foot, ankle and shin pain, from long-standing niggling pains to acute injuries.

Common conditions assessed include heel pain (plantar fasciitis), forefoot pain (Morton's neuroma), stress fractures, traumatic fractures, and shin splints (medial tibial stress syndrome). Alex can also help those with hypermobility, complex medical conditions, and can provide second opinions for foot and ankle pain or foot orthoses.

### Gait Analysis

Gait analysis is an assessment of how a patient walks and/or runs, an important part of understanding pain and injuries in the foot, ankle and shin. It can help identify whether the way a patient moves is contributing to their condition.

As part of the initial assessment, Alex films how the patient walks or runs, plays it back in slow motion, and explains what is happening and how it relates to the condition. A range of movement tests are also completed. This information is used to recommend specific footwear, determine whether foot orthoses may be helpful, or provide exercise rehabilitation.

Gait is reviewed over time and compared to previous recordings to check that the treatment plan is achieving the desired results.

### Exercise Rehabilitation

Getting back to activities often isn't just about reducing pain but also involves exercises to build strength or confidence with movement.

At the Barton clinic there is access to a fully equipped rehabilitation gym. Alex can provide simple exercises to get back to moving and walking through to comprehensive exercise rehabilitation and strength and conditioning programs.

Alex has access to strength testing equipment that can measure pulling and pushing strength, jump height and speed, and other tests. This helps measure asymmetries between left and right limbs, identify areas for improvement, and test whether an exercise program is working.

Patients leave with an exercise plan available on an app or printout, including what exercises to do, how many, how often, and video demonstrations.

### Foot Orthoses and Braces

Foot orthoses and braces are medical appliances placed in shoes that can support feet, ankles and legs to reduce pain and irritation. For the right person, they can help keep people on their feet, participating in exercise and sport, and promoting recovery. In some situations they can prevent injuries from returning.

#### Prefabricated Foot Orthoses

Pre-made orthoses modified for the patient and condition. Can often be fitted in the same consult or within a few days. Cheaper and good for more temporary conditions or trialling orthotic support, but less supportive than custom devices and with fewer design options.

#### Semi-Custom Foot Orthoses

Pre-made orthoses that can be modified to the patient's feet and condition, fitted by heating and moulding to the shape of the foot. A good middle ground especially for short-term conditions, substantially better than prefabricated at only a little more cost.

#### Custom Foot Orthoses

Made custom to the patient's foot. Involves taking a cast or 3D scan of the foot and sending it to a laboratory with a detailed prescription. More expensive but more supportive, designed specifically for the patient, their condition and their shoes. Last much longer than prefabricated devices. On average takes 2-3 weeks for custom orthoses to arrive from the laboratory.

#### Richie Brace (Ankle Foot Orthosis)

The Richie Brace is an Ankle Foot Orthosis (AFO), which is a foot orthosis with supports connecting it to the leg, making it more supportive than an orthosis alone. Suitable for more persistent or complex conditions such as severe ankle osteoarthritis, a progressively collapsing foot, foot drop, or any condition requiring more ankle support. Designed by a Podiatrist specifically so people could continue daily activities and walking and running.

### Footwear Prescription

Footwear is an important consideration for those with pain or those trying to prevent injury.

As part of the assessment, patients are asked to bring along the footwear they currently wear most. Combined with the injury assessment, advice and recommendations are provided for footwear appropriate for the condition and planned activities.

For athletic shoes, Alex writes a footwear prescription outlining the types of features and fitting required. Patients can take this to a sports footwear store with trained staff such as The Athlete's Foot, The Runners Shop, Momt, or Paddy Palin.

For casual and dress shoes, information is provided on the types of shoes, features and fitting, along with a list of recommended brands and stores.

### Nail and Skin Complaints

Alex is currently not able to accept any new patients for skin and nail complaints. Current patients are still able to continue to receive ongoing care.

Services available for existing patients include management of difficult-to-cut nails, ingrown toenails, corns, callous, fungal skin and nails, warts (verruca pedis), and diabetic foot assessments. Alex is not able to assist those suffering from foot ulcers or wounds.

### Sports Podiatry

With experience managing local, national and international athletes, runners of all abilities, and working on-field in sport, Alex treats all sports-related foot and ankle conditions.

Common conditions seen include tendon pain (including achilles, posterior tibial, peroneal, flexor and extensor tendons), stress and acute fractures, ankle sprains, joint pain including osteoarthritis, Morton's neuroma, plantar plate injury (turf toe), and foot and ankle bursitis.

Alex treats runners of all ages and abilities, from casual participants to competitive marathon runners, and is happy to see patients as the primary healthcare provider or as part of a healthcare and performance team.

At the first sports podiatry appointment, a thorough assessment of feet and ankles is performed, including walking and running analysis and a series of strength and mobility assessments. Running and injury history, current goals, and current training plan are all discussed. Any prior reports or radiology are reviewed. A detailed treatment plan is provided including guidance on modifying the training program if required.

### Running Injuries

Alex provides exercise rehabilitation to improve performance, footwear advice appropriate for the injury and running type, and management of skin and nail conditions that arise from running. Alex has experience working within healthcare and high performance teams including physiotherapists, medical specialists, strength and conditioning coaches, and running coaches.

## Conditions Treated

Alex diagnoses and manages all musculoskeletal conditions (muscle, bone, joint, tendon, ligament) of the foot, ankle and shin.

### Heel Pain (Plantar Fasciitis)

Heel pain is one of the most common foot and ankle conditions. Often called plantar fasciitis, people usually suffer pain when they first get up in the morning or after a period of rest, which improves with walking. As the condition progresses, pain can start coming on when walking or standing and be worse the next day.

Symptoms usually start building slowly with no identifiable cause. For those whose pain doesn't follow this pattern, it's likely caused by another condition such as bone or fat pad bruising, and in rare cases a tear or stress fracture.

Common treatments include taping, stretching, strength exercises, footwear modification, and foot orthoses. Not everyone with heel pain needs foot orthoses. Prior treatments that haven't worked are useful information to help figure out the best next step. For most people, it's safe and preferable to stay active during treatment.

### Forefoot Pain

Common causes of forefoot pain include Morton's neuroma (irritation or scarring of nerves in between the long toe bones), bursitis (inflammation of soft tissue between long toe bones), joint synovitis (inflammation of the balls of the feet), plantar plate injury (injury to a ligament in a toe), and sesamoiditis (irritation of small bones under the big toe).

Symptoms vary greatly based on the cause and can range from aching to sharp pain to burning and tingling. Given the variety of different causes, an accurate assessment is important. Scans such as ultrasound or MRI can be ordered and interpreted if required.

Common treatments include prescribing specific footwear, padded insoles, and gait re-training to temporarily alter running style to reduce pain.

### Shin Splints

Shin splints describes pain on the front of the shins that generally occurs during or after sport or exercise. The most common cause is medial tibial stress syndrome, which is bone irritation of the inside aspect of the tibia bone.

Alex can assess and diagnose whether the patient has shin splints or another associated condition, watch them walk and run, review training, and develop a plan to reduce pain while continuing to exercise as much as possible. Many people benefit from leg strengthening. An MRI can be ordered if required to confirm the condition or rule out stress fractures.

### Stress Fractures

Stress fractures (also known as bone stress injuries or BSIs) occur when repeated forces placed on a bone over time exceed what it can tolerate. They commonly feel like pinpoint pain, can feel sharp or like a deep ache, hurt when pressure is put on the foot or ankle, get better with rest, and can have some swelling in the area.

They commonly occur when training is suddenly increased well beyond what the bone can recover from, or when the body's ability to repair bones is reduced due to long-term medications, hormone imbalances, rare medical conditions, or menopause.

All fractures require a period of immobilisation in a boot or cast followed by exercise rehabilitation. Alex can identify, diagnose and order imaging to confirm stress fractures, provide an immobilisation boot, determine when to come out of the boot, and provide complete rehabilitation including exercises to improve muscle and bone strength and return-to-sport planning.

For traumatic fractures caused by significant force such as a fall or car accident, Alex advises going to the emergency room at the local hospital, as many traumatic fractures require a specialist opinion. Alex can assist with complete rehabilitation after traumatic fractures.

### Tendon Pain

People with tendon pain typically experience pain when they first stand up, feeling sore and stiff until they start moving. Pain can return after sport or physical activity, especially with movements like jumping, sprinting or changing direction.

Common tendon areas affected include the back of the ankle (achilles tendon), inside of the ankle and foot (posterior tibial tendon), outside of the ankle and foot (peroneal tendon), the top of the foot (extensor tendons), the bottom of the foot (flexor tendons), and the heel (plantar fascia).

Tendons respond well to strength exercises and generally do not like complete rest. Depending on severity, it is often safe and beneficial to continue exercising with a small amount of pain.

The assessment includes movement tests to identify whether movement patterns are contributing to the condition, and ordering a scan if needed. The treatment plan commonly includes guidance on remaining active throughout recovery, exercises to restore comfort and strength, footwear prescription, and in-shoe padding or foot orthoses to reduce tendon irritability.

Adjunct treatments such as cortisone injections and extracorporeal shockwave therapy (ECSWT) are not provided in the clinic, but Alex has relationships with other providers who specialise in these treatments and can provide specific referrals when appropriate.

### Osteoarthritis

Osteoarthritis causes joint pain that is often quite stiff after not moving for a period of time, followed by pain and swelling with physical activity. Common joints affected in the foot and ankle include the ankle, midfoot, and balls of the feet (metatarsophalangeal joints).

It often occurs later in life or 10-20 years after suffering from an injury or trauma. Alex can determine whether osteoarthritis is present clinically or by organising required scans. Treatment includes finding approaches that reduce strain on the affected joint, such as specific footwear and referral to a GP for anti-inflammatories, followed by exercises to strengthen the joint over time. Guidance is provided on remaining active and returning to sport or activity throughout the process.

### Other Conditions Treated

Knee-cap pain, altered walking patterns, recurrent sports injuries, paediatric sports injuries, paediatric foot and ankle conditions, complex cases, conditions pre and post-orthopaedic surgery, hypermobility and Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (EDS), rheumatological conditions including rheumatoid arthritis, low-risk diabetes, flat feet, and high-arched feet.

## Podiatry FAQs

#### What is podiatry?

In Australia, podiatry is a field of allied health focused on the treatment of all conditions of the foot and ankle, including pain and injury, skin and nail conditions, biomechanical and gait analysis, dermatological conditions, diabetes, wounds, and autoimmune conditions such as rheumatoid arthritis. Podiatry also addresses conditions of the whole lower limb where gait may be contributing, such as knee-cap pain.

#### What is a podiatrist?

A podiatrist is someone trained in the field of podiatry and granted a licence by the Australian Health Practitioner Regulation Authority (AHPRA) to use this title.

#### What can a podiatrist treat?

Common conditions include plantar fasciitis, neuroma, bursitis, achilles tendon pain, ankle pain, midfoot pain, bunions, shin splints, joint pain, sesamoid pain, stiffness in feet, hypermobile or flat feet, high-arched feet, fungal nail infections, warts, and diabetes management. In addition to standard podiatrist skills, Alex can test patient strength using specialised equipment, prescribe exercises for the leg, knee and whole lower limb, prescribe strength and conditioning programs, and provide return-to-sport plans after foot and ankle injuries.

#### Is a podiatrist a doctor?

In Australia, a podiatrist is not a trained medical practitioner and podiatry is not a medical specialty. Most podiatrists in Australia do not use the title of doctor.

#### What is chiropody?

Chiropody is the original name for the field of treating foot and ankle conditions. In Australia, there is no difference between chiropody and podiatry. The field expanded and is now known as podiatry.

## Foot Orthoses FAQs

#### How are orthotics made?

Custom orthotics are made by taking a mould or 3D scan of the foot and sending it to a laboratory. The laboratory uses the mould and specifications to make a device customised to the patient, which arrives approximately 2-3 weeks later. Semi-custom orthotics are pre-made and modified by heating and moulding to the shape of the foot. Prefabricated orthotics are pre-made and not significantly modifiable.

#### Will foot orthotics help my foot pain?

Foot orthoses can help a lot of conditions in the foot, ankle and knee, but they are not a good option for everyone. All appointments include a discussion of all treatment options including whether foot orthoses are worthwhile.

#### I have a flat foot. Do I need orthotics?

Not necessarily. Research shows that flat feet by themselves do not mean a person will develop pain or injury. The main reason to use orthotics for flat feet is when the patient is suffering from pain or an injury that would benefit from them.

#### What is the difference between hard and soft orthotics?

Hard shell orthotics are generally made from plastic, hold their shape well, are easier to fit in a wider range of shoes, and can be more easily repaired. They are used for most injuries and foot pain. Soft shell orthotics are generally made from high-density foam and are better suited to preventing foot wounds and corns or callouses, or for patients who find hard shells uncomfortable.

#### How long do foot orthotics last?

For harder shell orthotics with average use, 5-7 years is a reasonable expectation, though top covers may need replacing during that time. For softer shell orthotics, approximately 1-3 years. There is a manufacturer's warranty for the first 6 months for any faults.

#### Can I wear my orthotics straight away?

Orthotics should be worn in slowly. The recommended approach is 1 hour on day one, 2 hours on day two, and so on until wearing them all day comfortably. It is best not to go for long walks, runs or play sport in orthotics until they can be worn all day with no issues.

#### Do I have to wear orthotics for the rest of my life?

For most people, no. Most people do not get significant benefits from wearing orthotics longer than 1-2 years. There are exceptions including people with osteoarthritis, or conditions where a foot starts to flatten in adulthood, or people who simply feel a lot more comfortable wearing them.

#### My orthotics are uncomfortable. What do I do?

Orthotics are designed to be modifiable. At the fitting appointment, modifications can be made on-site to address any points of pressure or irritation. If modifications are needed after taking orthotics home, these are included in the cost. If orthotics were issued by another podiatrist and are uncomfortable, Alex can provide a second opinion and make any modifications required.
